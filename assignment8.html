Final Project: An Espresso Knock Box

Concept:
Being an amateur barista and all-around coffee connoisseur, last year I bought myself a De'Longhi combination coffee and espresso maker. What I'd been missing, however, was a convenient way to remove and collect my coffee grounds... until now!



Espresso knock box components:

  • Knock box (made of PLA)
  • Knock bar (steel rod covered in food-safe rubber exterior)
  • Sticky "feet' (to adhere to the bottom exterior of the knockbox and keep it from moving when in use)
  • A molded and casted food-safe silicone part (to place at the bottom interior of the knock box and absorb heat from hot coffee grounds)






Use case:
The knock box is a super practical kitchen tool that simplifies the art of coffeemaking and makes it more environmentally friendly by incorporating a way to easily compost our coffee grounds. On the retail market, a knock box can cost anywhere from $15 to $100 depdening on the brand, quality, and grade of materials used to produce it. For comparison, the materials for my DIY knock box cost less than $15, and I'm very happy with the quality and finish! This new tool also makes it easier to remove espresso grounds from the portafilter, saving me from knocking the grounds into the garbage can (so wasteful!) By collecting my coffee grounds in the new knock box, I've started a new "tiny habit" of composting them and can use the compost to enrich my garden soil. In addition, coffee grounds can be an effective, environmentally-friendly fertiizer for certain plants and flowers, and they can serve as a helpful natural pesticide to mitigate garden slugs and snails!

After landing on the preliminary design idea, I started creating my high level project plan. Below is the list of digital fabrication techniques that I planned to use for my final project - and a note about which ones I ended up using for the final product:
1. Rhino - planned and imcorporated
2. Grasshopper - planned but NOT incorporated
3. 3D printing - planned and incorporated (using 3 different printers during prototyping!)
4. Interface fits/intersecting parts (i.e., interface between the knock box and the knock bar) - planned and incorporated
5. Stretch goal: Molding and casting the "grippy" part on the bottom of the box in silicone

- planned and incorporated

Contingency incorporated into final design during the testing phase - purchased a stock part for the knock bar - this pivot to a stock part was part of my project proposal. In this case, I purchased a knock bar with a steel center and rubber exterior to increase the overall strength of the design given that a significant force can be exerted on the knock bar when someone tries to remove coffee grounds from the portafilter!
